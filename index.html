Bluebikes Usage Analysis in Boston

Overall Introduction

Bluebikes has become an important part of transportation in Boston by providing a convenient and environmentally friendly alternative to cars and buses. However, Bluebikes usage is not the same across all riders, times, and locations. Some trips are more likely to happen during commuting hours, while others may reflect leisure or recreational travel. In addition, station activity can vary based on where the station is located and whether the rider is a member or a casual user. This project studies these differences to better understand how Bluebikes fits into Boston’s everyday mobility system.

Analyzing Bluebikes usage provides insight into commuting behavior, mobility trends, and how residents interact with shared transportation systems. High usage during certain hours may indicate commuting demand, while differences between weekdays and weekends may reflect changes in work and leisure patterns. Understanding these trends can help improve system efficiency and enhance the overall user experience.

This topic is important because identifying temporal, spatial, and user-type patterns can help improve station placement, resource allocation, and transportation planning. By examining when people ride, where trips are concentrated, and how member behavior differs from casual rider behavior, we can better understand the structure of bike-share usage in the city and support better decision-making for urban planning and sustainability efforts.

Introduction to the Data

Our project uses the Bluebikes Comprehensive Trip Histories dataset, which contains detailed records of bike trips taken in Boston. The data is publicly available and is provided in multiple files across different years. Each dataset ranges from approximately 3,000 to 15,000 rows and includes around 15 attributes.

The dataset includes important variables such as trip duration, start and end stations, timestamps, bike ID, and user type. In addition, it contains rider information such as birth year and gender. These attributes allow us to analyze how Bluebikes are used across time, location, and different user groups.

Before conducting our analysis, we remove rows with missing values to ensure data quality, as the dataset is already relatively clean. Using this data, we focus on understanding temporal patterns such as peak usage hours, spatial patterns such as high-traffic stations, and differences in behavior between member and casual riders.

Heatmap: Trips by Hour and Day of Week

This heatmap shows trip frequency across each hour of the day and day of the week, split by membership type, gender, and age group. Members show a clear pattern, with concentrated activity during the 8am and 5–6pm rush hours on weekdays. Non-members ride more evenly throughout the day, with heavier activity on weekend afternoons. The gender split mirrors this divide; male subscribers drive the weekday peaks while the Unknown category, which consists almost entirely of unregistered non-members, reflects the flatter leisure curve. Among age groups, Millennials and Gen X members account for the sharpest commuter spikes, while younger and older riders show more diffuse patterns across the week.

CandleStick Chart: Trip Duration by Hour and Day

This CandleStick chart shows the distribution of trip durations across hours of the day and days of the week, with toggles for membership type and the option to cap the y-axis to filter outliers. Most Bluebikes trips fall between 7 and 19 minutes regardless of timing. Weekend afternoons and late-night hours produce wider interquartile ranges and higher means, consistent with recreational riding. Switching to the Non-Member view reveals higher medians and a wider spread across all hours and days.

Bar Chart: Most and Least Active Stations

The interactive bar chart ranks Bluebikes stations by total activity (departures + arrivals). South Station leads with 9,218 trips, well ahead of second-place MIT at Mass Ave/Amherst St with 8,103 trips. The remaining stations decline gradually from Harvard Square at 5,877 trips down to TD Garden at 4,744. Stations near major transit hubs and dense academic or commercial corridors dominate the top of the list, reflecting the importance of high foot-traffic locations in driving bike share usage. Toggling to Least Active reveals the quieter residential and peripheral stations.

Interactive Map: Station Activity Across Boston

We use Folium to make an interactive map of the greater Boston area covering stations across Boston, Cambridge, and Somerville. The heat intensity indicates trip volume at each station. Users can toggle between All Riders, Subscribers, and Customers using the layer control to compare ridership patterns. A time slider lets users go through each hour of the day in order to investage how demand shifts over time. Clicking any station marker reveals the station name, trip count, rider type breakdown, and average trip duration. I noticed that the station with the highest trip count is South Station, which is a major transportation hub in Boston. This station likely serves a l arge number of commuters and travelers, contributing to its high usage. The map also shows that stations located in central areas of Boston, Cambridge, and Somerville tend to have higher trip volumes.

Chord Diagram: Station-to-Station Flow

Next, we have a chord diagram mapping the volume of trips between the top stations, where ribbon width encodes the number of trips exchanged. The Cambridge and MIT cluster shows the densest internal flow, with MIT Mass Ave, MIT Stata Center, and Kendall T forming a tight triangle. South Station connects heavily outward toward Back Bay and the downtown financial district stations. Cross-river ribbons between Cambridge and Boston are thinner, suggesting most riders stay within their local area.

Scatter Plot: Riding Efficiency by Group

Finally, this is a scatter plot of distance against trip duration with solid regression lines showing the average efficiency trend per group. Members cluster below the 10 km/h reference line. Non-members scatter above it, frequently taking 30 to 40 minutes for distances members cover in under 10, which is at least partly explained by Bluebikes pricing. Non-members are charged a flat rate covering the first 30 minutes, giving casual riders a financial incentive to maximize their ride time rather than return the bike quickly. Switching the color grouping to Neighborhood reveals that Cambridge and MIT trips hug the efficient lower band, benefiting from dedicated infrastructure, while Fenway and Kenmore rides show considerably more spread.

Summary and Future Work

In this visualization analysis, we explore how Bluebikes are used in Boston across time, location, and rider type. We examine temporal patterns such as peak usage hours, spatial patterns such as high-traffic stations, and differences between member and casual riders. These visualizations help highlight how Bluebikes fits into everyday transportation and how usage varies across different conditions.

One limitation of this analysis is that the data is limited to a specific time period, and usage patterns may change over time. In addition, external factors such as weather and seasonal variation are not included in this analysis. Future work could include incorporating multiple years of data, adding weather data, and performing a more detailed time series analysis to better understand long-term trends in Bluebikes usage.

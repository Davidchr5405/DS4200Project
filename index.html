Bluebikes Usage Analysis in Boston

Overall Introduction

Bluebikes has become an important part of transportation in Boston by providing a convenient and environmentally friendly alternative to cars and buses. However, Bluebikes usage is not the same across all riders, times, and locations. Some trips are more likely to happen during commuting hours, while others may reflect leisure or recreational travel. In addition, station activity can vary based on where the station is located and whether the rider is a member or a casual user. This project studies these differences to better understand how Bluebikes fits into Boston’s everyday mobility system.

Analyzing Bluebikes usage provides insight into commuting behavior, mobility trends, and how residents interact with shared transportation systems. High usage during certain hours may indicate commuting demand, while differences between weekdays and weekends may reflect changes in work and leisure patterns. Understanding these trends can help improve system efficiency and enhance the overall user experience.

This topic is important because identifying temporal, spatial, and user-type patterns can help improve station placement, resource allocation, and transportation planning. By examining when people ride, where trips are concentrated, and how member behavior differs from casual rider behavior, we can better understand the structure of bike-share usage in the city and support better decision-making for urban planning and sustainability efforts.

Introduction to the Data

Our project uses the Bluebikes Comprehensive Trip Histories dataset, which contains detailed records of bike trips taken in Boston. The data is publicly available and is provided in multiple files across different years. Each dataset ranges from approximately 3,000 to 15,000 rows and includes around 15 attributes.

The dataset includes important variables such as trip duration, start and end stations, timestamps, bike ID, and user type. In addition, it contains rider information such as birth year and gender. These attributes allow us to analyze how Bluebikes are used across time, location, and different user groups.

Using this data, we focus on understanding temporal patterns such as peak usage hours, spatial patterns such as high-traffic stations, and differences in behavior between member and casual riders.

Overview of the Unemployment rate

First, we present an overview of the unemployment rate for all 50 states. Alaska is the one with the highest unemployment rate, which may due to the seasonal business in the state. The effectiveness of labor market policies and governance structures can influence unemployment rates. Washington, D.C., being the seat of government, may experience fluctuations in employment related to government spending and policies. Mississippi and Louisiana have economies that are heavily reliant on sectors such as agriculture, manufacturing, and energy. Economic downturns or fluctuations in these industries can impact employment opportunities. Connecticut, South Dakota and Virginia have the lowest unemplyment rate. These states often have diverse economies with strong sectors such as technology, healthcare, finance, and tourism. A diverse economy can provide more resilience to economic downturns.

The relationship between unemployment rate and other social factors

Moreover, we also want to examine the relationship between unemployment rate and other social factors in the data. All features are numerical thus we firts create a correlation plot to examine whether there are linear relationship between unemployment rate with other social factors. Notice that if the correlation is small, it only means that there is no potential linear relationship between two features.

Based on the correlation plot, among all the features, poverty has the strongest correlation with the unemployment rate since they are both related to the economics. The percentage of single parents is the second important feature which inidicates the stability of family is also strongly related to the job market. The increase of unemployment rate may also related to the increase of violent crime and murder rate but has a negative relationship with education indicators, like the percentage of people who go to high school and college. The only variable that has no direct linear relation with the unemployment rate is Metropolitan, which means the percent of residents living in a metropolitan area.
